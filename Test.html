Understanding Standard Precautions

Standard Precautions represent the minimum infection prevention practices that apply to all patient care, regardless of suspected or confirmed infection status. These evidence-based practices are designed to protect healthcare personnel and prevent transmission among patients.

Core Components

Standard Precautions include hand hygiene, proper PPE selection, safe injection practices, safe handling of contaminated equipment, and respiratory hygiene/cough etiquette. These practices are based on the assumption that blood, body fluids, secretions, and excretions may contain transmissible pathogens.

Key Pathogens at a Glance

Transmission Routes
Transmitted through contact with infectious blood and body fluids. Most commonly through needlestick injuries, mucous membrane exposure, and non-intact skin contact.

Survivability on Surfaces
Can survive on surfaces for up to 7 days at room temperature. Remains infectious during this period.

Vaccine Availability
Highly effective vaccine available. Healthcare workers should receive the complete 3-dose series and verify immunity through post-vaccination testing.

HCV is primarily bloodborne and often associated with needlestick injuries. No vaccine is currently available; prevention and follow-up testing are essential.

Occupational HIV transmission risk is lower than HBV but clinically significant. Immediate reporting and rapid post-exposure prophylaxis pathways are key.

Additional bloodborne pathogens may appear in clinical settings. Consistent PPE use, sharps safety, and universal precautions remain the strongest prevention strategy.
